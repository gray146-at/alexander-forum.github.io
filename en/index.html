Our mission

The Alexander Forum for Reflection & Resilience is an independent, non-profit Think & Do Tank based in Vienna. We combine societal observation, strategic thinking, and practical experience to open new perspectives on change, crises, and social cohesion.

We see resilience as a shared task across politics, science, culture, and society. Personal and societal crises are interwoven: when people are under pressure, they contribute less – and unresolved tensions weaken the whole.

Themes

Our approach is interdisciplinary – from climate and conflict to technology and security, psychology and medicine. We work openly, name uncertainties transparently, and publish analyses and briefs that provide orientation, highlight omissions, and suggest solutions.

  • Resilience & societal stress tests
    Between adaptation, exhaustion, and renewal.
  • Information & trust
    Communication, fear, polarization, and responsibility in the digital age.
  • Technology & risk
    How progress reshapes stability and security.
  • Identity & cohesion
    Belonging, trauma, and what holds a community together.

Approach

We foster reflection over noise and a dialogue that can hold contradictions and take multiple perspectives seriously. The Forum invites discourse and looks for ways to turn constructive thinking into shared action.

Our work remains practice-oriented – in analyses and syntheses, in briefings for organizations, and in workshops that enable exchange and strengthen resilience as a shared stance.

Foundational Dossier: Resilience – Interdisciplinary Analysis and Future Competency

Alexander Forum for Reflection & Resilience (Vienna) · October 10, 2025 · Version 1.1

Resilience is the ability to withstand crises, recover, and grow from them – conceived biologically, psychologically, socially, and systemically. This dossier explains why resilience is part of our name and how it can be fostered in practice: from early attachment and organizational culture to neuroplasticity, community resilience – including digital resilience as a new cultural technique.

Why "Resilience" in our name?

For us, resilience is a principle of shaping – creating conditions under which people and communities do not merely endure but flourish.

  • – Dynamic concept: Resilience is learnable and shapeable.
  • – Biopsychosocial: Person × Environment – protective factors can overcompensate risks.
  • – Practice-oriented: Exercises, organizational transfer, community and system resilience.
  • – Digital: Information overload, social comparison & "Digital Resilience Literacy".

Our Alexandrian Role Models

The name "Alexander" is our program – as an attitude, not personality cult. The figures assembled here – men and women with "Alexander" connections – show different facets of resilience and reflection: systemic thinking, innovative spirit, moral steadfastness, institutional stability, and personal exploration.

Hypatia of Alexandria

Education as Lighthouse

Philosopher, mathematician, and teacher in late antique Alexandria. Hypatia stands for the idea that education and knowledge transmission create cultural resilience: critical thinking, dialogic capacity, and the passing on of rational tools – even under pressure.

  • Education as cultural immune system
  • Spaces for critique stabilize communities
  • Knowledge outlasts short-term storms

Hypatia of Alexandria – Education as Lighthouse

In brief: Philosopher, mathematician, teacher; symbol of rational thought and teaching tradition in turbulent times.

Hypatia taught at one of antiquity's most significant knowledge centers. Her importance lies less in a single work than in practice: teaching, discussing, passing on methods. In phases of social tension, precisely this protects – shared standards, fairness in argument, verifiable reasons – from sliding into myth and violence. Education becomes a societal resilience achievement.

Our lessons
  • Invest in education: It works slowly but sustainably.
  • Remain capable of critique: Debate culture is stability culture.
  • Mediate rather than preach: Methods strengthen self-efficacy.

Alexandra David-Néel

Courage at the Edges

Traveler, author, Tibet researcher. She crossed boundaries – geographic, cultural, social – and combined adventurous spirit with inner discipline. Her life shows: Resilience emerges when curiosity, self-leadership, and respect for the foreign come together.

  • Exploration strengthens self-efficacy
  • Inner order in uncertain environments
  • Cultural sensitivity as resource

Alexandra David-Néel – Courage at the Edges

In brief: Traveler, Tibet researcher, author – an example of personal robustness and cultural openness.

David-Néel combined adventure, asceticism, and precise observation. Her boundary crossings – partly disguised, often under real risks – show how inner order (rituals, breath, focus) and respect for the Other lead to resilient action. Exploration without hubris is a school of self-leadership.

Our lessons
  • Train self-leadership (routine, breath, sleep, movement).
  • Cultivate curiosity, suspend judgment.
  • Assess risk, respect boundaries, yet go.

Alexandra Kollontai

Shaping Solidarity

Politician, feminist, diplomat – one of the first women in high state offices. Kollontai fought for social security, women's rights, and institutional reforms. She stands for the insight: Collective resilience grows when structures enable fairness.

  • Resilience needs institutions & networks
  • Justice is prevention
  • Persistence against resistance

Alexandra Kollontai – Shaping Solidarity

In brief: Politician, feminist, diplomat; one of the first women as minister and ambassador. Focus: social rights, protection from poverty, participation.

Kollontai stands for the insight that individual resilience hits limits when structures are unjust. Her agenda – childcare, social security, women workers' rights – understands resilience as a collective task. Institutions that consider care work and vulnerability increase the crisis robustness of the entire society.

Our lessons
  • Resilience policy is social policy: Security prevents crisis consequences.
  • Make institutions fair – this creates trust.
  • Negotiate persistently: Change is a process, not a sprint.

Alexander the Great

Strategy & Adaptability

Military leader and state founder with enormous impact – and ambivalences. His campaigns spread knowledge and networking but were also violent. Lesson: Leadership in uncertainty needs speed and translation – but always with ethics checks.

  • Deciding under uncertainty
  • Cultural translation work
  • Reflect power, limit damage

Alexander the Great – Strategy & Adaptability

In brief: Brilliant strategist with ambivalent record: networking and knowledge circulation – but also violence and expansion.

Instructive is the handling of uncertainty: quick deciding, learning in the field, cultural translation work (administration, languages, elites). Equally instructive is the limit: Power that is not reflected produces new vulnerability. Resilient leadership combines courage with self-limitation.

Our lessons
  • Learn quickly, correct cleanly.
  • Take local contexts seriously.
  • Build ethics checks into strategies.

Alexander Solzhenitsyn

Truth as Resistance

Camp survivor and author whose work centers witnessing, meaning, and responsibility. He reminds us: Psychological resilience after trauma needs truthfulness – personally and societally.

  • Memory protects from repetition
  • Meaning orientation stabilizes
  • Witnessing creates culture of responsibility

Alexander Solzhenitsyn – Truth as Resistance

In brief: Writer and witness of political violence; his work grapples with meaning, memory, and accountability.

Resilience here means re-narrating destroyed realities. Witnessing protects from repression, meaning provides direction, and public debates create spaces for healing. Solzhenitsyn reminds: Without truth, resilience remains facade.

Our lessons
  • Memory politics is health prevention.
  • Strengthen sources of meaning (values, belonging, practice).
  • Institutionally secure witnessing.

Alexander Fleming

Mistakes as Treasure

Discoverer of penicillin. His openness to the unexpected – and a contaminated petri dish – became a medical breakthrough. Resilience here means: error culture, observational acuity, rapid translation.

  • Make serendipity productive
  • Error-friendly research culture
  • Connection of lab & practice

Alexander Fleming – Mistakes as Treasure

In brief: Discoverer of penicillin; example of serendipitous innovation through careful observation.

A "botched" petri dish led to discovery with global impact. The message: Create conditions where chance can become useful – with error culture, rapid prototyping, and bridges between lab and application.

Our lessons
  • Make mistakes visible and evaluate them.
  • From observation to prototype – without detours.
  • Scientific humility as strength.

Alexander von Humboldt

Making Connections Visible

Natural scientist and systems thinker. He connected ecology, climate, data, and culture – observed locally, understood globally. His lesson: Resilience begins with seeing interactions and long-term horizons.

  • Systems understanding as basis
  • Interdisciplinarity increases adaptability
  • Think long-term, act short

Alexander von Humboldt – Connections & Systems Thinking

In brief: Natural scientist and world thinker; connected ecology, climate, data, and culture.

Humboldt shows how resilience is conceived beyond the individual: in ecosystems, institutions, and practices. Who recognizes interactions avoids short-circuit politics and builds robust paths – scientifically grounded, socially embedded, long-term.

Our lessons
  • Institutionalize interdisciplinarity.
  • Couple local action with global perspective.
  • Long-term horizons against pure efficiency logic.
